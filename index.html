THE HISTORY

The Mercury programme, which was the United States' first human spaceflight initiative, saw the beginning of human research into astronauts. The programme, which ran from 1959 to 1963, was carried out by NASA, the American space agency. Alan B. Shepard Jr., the first American to travel to space when he boarded the Freedom 7 spacecraft on May 5, 1961, carried out the first astronaut study. Shepard carried out a number of experiments during his 15-minute suborbital flight, testing the spacecraft's equipment and assessing the effects of weightlessness on his body. The Mercury program also included other notable astronauts, such as John Glenn, who became the first American to orbit the Earth, and Gordon Cooper, who conducted the longest solo spaceflight in history at the time. It provided valuable insights into the physiological and psychological effects of space travel on humans, as well as the technical challenges of designing and building spacecraft capable of carrying humans into space.

MOON LANDING

On July 20, 1969, American astronauts Neil Armstrong (1930-2012) and Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin (1930-) became the first humans ever to land on the moon. About six-and-a-half hours later, Armstrong became the first person to walk on the moon. As he took his first step, Armstrong famously said, "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." The Apollo 11 mission occurred eight years after President John F. Kennedy (1917-1963) announced a national goal of landing a man on the moon by the end of the 1960s. Apollo 17, the final manned moon mission, took place in 1972
